Field Notes

Short dispatches from the intersection of mud, models and megawatts.

Note 01 · Water

What 164 water points taught me about data

In the Freetown Complex, a wrong guess isn't a bad cell in a spreadsheet — it's a dry hole a community paid for. Geophysics taught me that data only matters when someone stakes something on it. The resistivity surveys behind our groundwater maps pushed the strike rate to 70% in rock where drilling is a gamble, and every strike was the same lesson: collect the data before the drill bit, not after.

I carry that into every ESG analysis and every AI framework I build now. The question is never "what does the data say?" — it's "who is about to spend money, or drink water, based on what I claim it says?"

Note 02 · Scenarios

Why I wrote a pessimistic future on purpose

My Wageningen thesis ended with "Dirty Delta" — a deliberately dark storyline for Africa's coastal waters by mid-century. Model output rarely moves a policymaker; a story they can picture does. Turning regression coefficients on income, urbanisation and agriculture into a narrative was the most useful translation work I've done.

The lesson survived the thesis: if your analysis can't be retold in a corridor after the meeting, it doesn't exist. I now write the storyline first and let it demand the numbers it needs.

Note 03 · Sustainable AI

Every prompt has a footprint — so what?

A watt-hour means nothing to most people. "This answer boiled a fifth of a kettle" does. At the Digital Society School I'm building decision tools for the Dutch Ministry of Finance around exactly that translation — because the gap between the most and least efficient model on the same task can exceed an order of magnitude, and nobody puts that in front of the person typing the prompt.

The point is not AI guilt. It's the same soft-path thinking I learned in water management: before you build a bigger pipe — or call a bigger model — ask whether the demand itself can be smarter. Try the widget above; that's the argument, running live.
